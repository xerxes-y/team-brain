Two tools, two halves of the same problem

AI-assisted teams lose knowledge twice. Before a change: the assistant builds the wrong thing because requirements live only in chat. After a change: the reasoning behind it evaporates — the spec was written, the gotchas weren't, and three sprints later someone re-asks or silently breaks it.

Before — OpenSpec

Spec-driven development

Slash-command workflow (/opsx:propose → apply → archive) that turns a vague prompt into a proposal, spec scenarios, a design, and a task list under openspec/changes/ — so the AI assistant builds the right thing.

Unit of knowledge: one change, authored deliberately, by a developer, living in the repo.

After — team-brain

Role-aware org memory

Connectors (Jira, Confluence, PRs, IDE) + deliberate team_capture pull the team's reasoning into one searchable store. team_assist answers with citations, in the asker's role voice — PO, developer, or tester.

Unit of knowledge: everything the team learned, captured as it happens, searchable forever.

The logical case — step by step

Not a feature list. A chain of premises — check each one against your own team. If they hold, the conclusion isn't optional.

Premise 1 — verifiable in your last sprint

Every ticket produces reasoning that is in nobody's artifact: why this approach, which options were rejected, which gotcha almost shipped a bug. Test it: the last time someone asked "why is it done this way?" — was the answer in Jira, or in someone's head?

Premise 2 — check your Slack

That reasoning gets asked for again: by the tester reconciling expected vs actual, by the next dev touching the code, by every new hire. Each re-ask interrupts two people; when the person who knew rotates off, the answer is gone entirely.

Premise 3 — you already ran this experiment

Manual documentation doesn't fix it. Writing docs costs the most effort at exactly the moment incentive is lowest — the work is done. Your company has Confluence. People still re-ask. That's not a discipline failure; it's the predictable outcome of the incentive structure.

Premise 4 — vocabulary mismatch

The person who needs the knowledge uses different words than the person who produced it. The tester asks "riskiest edge case"; the dev wrote "shared clearing account". Keyword search — Confluence's, Jira's — returns nothing. Zero shared words, same fact.

∴ Therefore

If capture must be near-zero-effort at the moment knowledge exists (from P3), it has to be automatic connectors + a one-command push — not a docs habit. If retrieval must cross role vocabularies (from P4), it has to be search by meaning with role-aware ranking — not keyword search. Add citations (so answers can be trusted) and fail-closed ACL — access control that hides anything it isn't sure the asker may see — and you've derived team-brain's design. It isn't a product category we invented — it's the conclusion of the premises.

And the objections, honestly: "We have Confluence/Jira" — they hold artifacts; P1 is about the reasoning between artifacts, and P3 explains why they didn't solve it. "The other team has OpenSpec" — specs record decisions made before building; the counterparty-account gotcha below was found during building and made no spec. "Nobody will capture" — most capture is passive (connectors over what you already produce); the deliberate part is one command, and yes: if a team does neither, the store stays empty. That's the one honest precondition, stated on the tin.

The two objections from our own demo — answered with a walkthrough

When OpenSpec was demoed here, two objections came up. Both are real. Both are knowledge problems — which is exactly the half team-brain solves. (The coordination half — merging files, assigning work — stays git and Jira; no tool should pretend otherwise.)

Objection 1 · raised at the demo

"Two people on related tasks can't use it together"

True — an OpenSpec change is one folder with effectively one author. Person B can't see person A's in-flight decisions until the spec merges, so they diverge or duplicate. team-brain's answer: everyone's commits, captures, and specs land in one store tagged to the ticket. B queries A's decisions before starting — nobody waits, nobody guesses, nobody is interrupted.

Objection 2 · raised at the demo

"Mid-task I realize another task must come first"

True — tasks.md is a pre-implementation checklist; a dependency discovered during the work has no home in it. team-brain's answer: capture the constraint the moment it's found — one command, or just a TODO/commit message the connectors pick up automatically. Whoever touches either task next finds it, cited, instead of re-discovering it.

Three integration points, zero workflow change

OpenSpec stays the authoring tool. team-brain stays the memory. Nobody on either team changes how they work.

1 · Ingest

openspec connector

Walks openspec/changes/: proposal.md → the why (PO boosts it), specs/*.md → requirements & scenarios (tester gold), design.md → the how (dev boosts it). Jira keys in the change → ticket: tags, repo visibility → acl: tags, file paths → src: citations.

2 · Recall

explore → team_assist

One line in the OpenSpec workflow instructions: before proposing, ask team_assist("what do we know about <area>?"). New proposals start from past specs, PR reasoning, and captured chats — not a blank page.

3 · Bridge

scenarios → test_plan

OpenSpec's spec scenarios are almost ready-made test input. Ingested and tagged, team_test_plan merges them with the developer's implementation gotchas — the tester gets both sides without chasing anyone.

# the whole integration, from the team-brain side:
python3 -c "from teambrain.connectors.openspec import sync; \
  print(sync('/path/to/repo', namespace='team-eng'))"

# and one line added to the OpenSpec team's workflow instructions:
/opsx:explore  →  first call team_assist(area, role="developer")

Why the spec alone isn't enough

A spec records decisions — and only the ones a developer chose to write down before building. The expensive knowledge is what surfaces during the work:

What the spec captured

  • Match settlement rows per custodian bank
  • Scenario: Bank A sends a transfer-id in the comment
  • Design: extend the existing type-8 finalize flow

What only the chat had

  • ⚠ The source account is always the shared clearing account — match on the counterparty or Banks B/C silently mis-book
  • Bank C's lookup endpoint is still an open question for the BA
  • Why the team rejected comment-based matching for B/C

OpenSpec can't hold the right column — it's a planning artifact, not a capture system. team-brain can't produce the left column — it's a memory, not a planning discipline. The tester needs both columns in one answer. That's the integration.

What this means for a multi-team company

Teams on OpenSpec

Keep the workflow. Point the connector at the repo once — archived changes become searchable, cited, ACL'd memories. Their specs now answer other teams' questions too.

Teams without it

Jira, Confluence, PRs, and IDE capture already feed the brain. No spec discipline required to get value — adopt OpenSpec later (or never) without re-platforming.

PO & testers everywhere

The roles OpenSpec can't reach — its artifacts live in a git repo they don't work in — get the same knowledge through team_draft_ticket and team_test_plan.

The pitch in one line: OpenSpec is per-team planning hygiene; team-brain is the shared memory layer across teams and roles — and it makes every OpenSpec team's specs worth more, not less.

Beyond the IDE — answer other teams in Microsoft Teams

The brain isn't tied to the IDE. The same read path — search, ACL, role rerank, synthesis — sits behind a thin, read-only Teams bridge, so anyone in a channel can ask and get the same cited answer. With synthesis pointed at an internal LLM, nothing leaves the network.

@team-brain why do we match on the counterparty?→ webhook (HMAC-verified)→ team_assist→ ACL fail-closed→ internal LLM cites→ answer in the thread

Internal LLM: config, not code

Synthesis is a pluggable seam — point it at any OpenAI-compatible internal endpoint (company gateway, vLLM, Ollama). Embeddings already run local. The whole pipeline stays in-house.

Read-only by design

The bridge only answers — it can't write memories, run connectors, or file tickets. Capture stays where the work happens (IDE, git, Jira); Teams is a question surface.

ACL makes it safe to open up

A Teams webhook can't know the asker's AD groups, so the ACL treats them as unknown: public memories only, fail-closed. Granting a channel more is an explicit, per-channel setting — never a default.

# ships in the repo — stdlib only, no Bot Framework SDK:
export TEAMBRAIN_TEAMS_SECRET=<token from the Teams outgoing webhook>
export TEAMBRAIN_NAMESPACE=team-eng
export TEAMBRAIN_SYNTH=teambrain.synth_openai:synth OPENAI_BASE_URL=http://llm.internal/v1
python3 -m teambrain.teams          # then: @team-brain as tester: what should I test for PROSET-9913?

Does it save LLM credit? — yes, by design

The expensive way to answer "why does this rule exist?" with AI is to let an agent re-explore the codebase — dozens of tool calls, a frontier model, every question, every asker, every session. team-brain's read path was designed around the opposite: search first, synthesize second. Retrieval is free; the only paid call is the last, smallest step — and even that one is optional.

1 · Finding knowledge costs zero credits

Embeddings run locally (sentence-transformers on CPU/GPU) and keyword search is Postgres BM25. The entire search-rank-filter pipeline — the hard part — never touches a paid API.

2 · Retrieval bounds the prompt

Synthesis reads the top-8 ranked snippets plus your question — roughly 2–4k tokens — not a repo dump. A prompt that small doesn't need a frontier model: a cheap or local model reads 8 snippets just fine.

3 · Many answers need no LLM at all

With no synthesis model configured, team_assist returns the ranked snippets with citations — extractive, zero tokens. For "where is this decided?" lookups, that's often the whole answer.

4 · Capture once, serve forever

An agent that re-derives context pays per asker, per session. A captured decision is distilled once (one small call — or a free offline heuristic) and then served to every future asker from the store.

Honest accounting: code-mining (GitLab connector) and capture distillation each cost one small call when an LLM is wired — and both fall back to offline heuristics without one. Those are one-time write-path costs, amortized over every future read. The structural point: credits scale with question volume, not codebase size — and the model tier scales with an 8-snippet prompt, not with "understand the whole repo."
